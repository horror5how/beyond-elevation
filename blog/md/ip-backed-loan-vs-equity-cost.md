# An IP-Backed Loan Costs $1.08M. The Same Raise as Equity Costs You $20M. The Founder Math Nobody Runs Before Their Next Round

Raise $4M as an IP-backed loan at 9% over three years and it costs you $1.08M in interest. Raise the same $4M by selling equity and it can cost you $20M at exit. Same cash in the bank. An 18x difference in what you pay for it. Most founders never run this comparison before their next round, and it is the single most expensive number they skip.

The IP-backed loan vs equity decision comes down to one question: do you have patents, a data asset, or a licensing stream a lender will price as collateral? If you do, debt is almost always cheaper than dilution. Here is the exact founder math, the tradeoffs nobody explains, and when equity still wins.

## IP-Backed Loan vs Equity: The $1.08M vs $20M Math

The cost of an IP-backed loan is the interest you pay. The cost of equity is the slice of your exit you give away forever. Founders treat these as the same category of decision. They are not close.

Run the numbers on a $4M raise:

**Path A, the IP-backed loan.** You borrow $4M against your patent portfolio or a proven licensing stream. Rates on IP-backed facilities sit in the 6 to 12% range depending on the strength of the asset. At 9% over three years, simple interest is $4M x 9% x 3 = $1.08M. You keep 100% of your company. The debt is repaid and the story ends.

**Path B, the equity round.** You sell 10% of the company at a $40M post-money valuation to raise the same $4M. Three years later the company exits at $200M. That 10% you sold is now worth $20M. The true cost of your $4M was never the paperwork. It was the $20M of exit value that walked out the door with your investors.

$1.08M against $20M. That is the number the term sheet never shows you, because the equity cost only becomes real on the day you can no longer change it.

## Why Founders Default to Equity Even When It Is the Expensive Option

Three reasons founders reach for equity first, and all three are fixable.

First, equity feels free. There is no monthly payment, no covenant, no repayment date. The cost is deferred to exit, so it does not show up in the cash flow model. Deferred cost reads as no cost. It is not.

Second, most founders do not know their IP is bankable. They think of patents as legal insurance, not as an asset a lender will lend against. The lending market disagrees. A growing number of specialist lenders now accept patents, trademarks, and contracted licensing revenue as collateral, and the volume of IP-backed and venture-debt facilities has climbed every year since 2022.

Third, nobody priced the asset. You cannot borrow against IP you have never valued. ## When Equity Still Wins Over an IP-Backed Loan

Debt is cheaper, but it is not always right. Be honest about the tradeoff.

An IP-backed loan has to be serviced. It needs either cash flow to cover the payments or an asset the lender can recover if you default. A pre-revenue company burning cash with no contracted licensing income is a poor fit for debt, no matter how strong the patents look on paper. Equity is patient capital. It does not ask for a payment next month.

Equity also buys more than money. A tier-one investor brings distribution, hiring, follow-on capital, and signal. If the round is the difference between a strategic partner and a stranger, the dilution can pay for itself. The mistake is not choosing equity. The mistake is choosing it by default, without pricing the debt alternative first.

The founders who win run both numbers. They raise a smaller equity round for the strategic value, then cover the rest with non-dilutive IP-backed capital. A $4M need becomes a $1.5M equity round plus a $2.5M loan, and the dilution is cut by more than half. That blend is only visible once your IP carries a valuation.

## FAQ

### Is an IP-backed loan cheaper than raising equity?

In most cases, yes. The cost of an IP-backed loan is capped at the interest you pay, often 6 to 12% per year. The cost of equity is the share of your exit you give away, which on a strong outcome can be 10 to 20 times the equivalent debt cost. Debt is cheaper whenever you have the cash flow or collateral to service it.

### Can you actually borrow against patents?

Yes. A specialist set of lenders accepts patents, trademarks, and contracted licensing revenue as collateral for IP-backed loans and venture debt. The requirement is an independent valuation of the asset and a clean ownership chain, which is why most founders need to price and tidy their IP before a lender will engage.

### What is the real cost of dilution vs debt?

Debt cost is the interest, a fixed and knowable number. Dilution cost is the future value of the equity you sold, which is unknown at the time of the raise and usually far larger at exit. The gap is invisible on raise day and painful on exit day, which is why the comparison has to be run before you sign.

### When should a founder still choose equity?

Choose equity when you lack the cash flow or collateral to service debt, or when a specific investor brings strategic value, distribution, hiring, or follow-on capital, that outweighs the dilution. The strongest play is often a blend: a smaller equity round for the strategic partner plus non-dilutive IP-backed capital for the rest.
