# The 7-Point IP Defensibility Test That Exposes Most Startup Portfolios

78% of VC-backed startups hold patents. Only 12% have portfolios that would survive a serious competitive challenge. The gap between those two numbers is where founders lose their valuation premium, their licensing revenue, and their exit multiple — without ever knowing it.

An IP defensibility assessment is the diagnostic that separates protected companies from exposed ones. Hayat Amin developed the IP Defensibility 7-Point Test after auditing portfolios across dozens of tech companies, and the results were consistent — most portfolios look impressive in a pitch deck and collapse under investor due diligence. Here is how it works, and why your portfolio probably fails it.

## What Is an IP Defensibility Assessment?

An IP defensibility assessment is a structured evaluation of whether your patent portfolio, trade secrets, and other IP assets create genuine competitive barriers — or merely create the appearance of protection. Unlike a standard IP audit that catalogues what you own, a defensibility assessment tests whether what you own actually works against real competitors in real markets.

Most founders confuse having patents with having protection. Filing a patent is a legal event. Building defensibility is a strategic one. The difference shows up in two places: the term sheet and the acquisition offer. Companies with patents are 10.2x more likely to secure early-stage funding — but only when the portfolio passes scrutiny.

## Why Do Most Startup IP Portfolios Fail the IP Defensibility Assessment?

Most startup IP portfolios fail because they were built reactively — filed after the product shipped, drafted by attorneys optimising for grant probability instead of commercial value, and structured without reference to the competitive landscape. Hayat Amin argues this is the single most expensive mistake in startup IP: paying $30K per patent for claims so narrow that no well-funded competitor would ever need to work around them.

The pattern repeats across industries. A founder files a provisional patent on their core innovation. Twelve months later, a patent attorney drafts claims that are technically accurate but commercially irrelevant — protecting the specific implementation rather than the competitive advantage. The patent grants. The founder celebrates. A well-resourced competitor enters the same market with a slightly different architecture that falls completely outside the claim scope.

That $30K patent created zero competitive barrier. The IP defensibility assessment would have caught this before the money was spent.

## What Are the 7 Points of the IP Defensibility Assessment?

The Hayat Amin IP Defensibility 7-Point Test evaluates your portfolio across seven dimensions that determine real-world competitive protection. Each point receives a pass or fail. Portfolios scoring below 5 out of 7 have structural weaknesses that will surface during fundraising due diligence, licensing negotiations, or acquisition conversations.

**Point 1 — Claim Breadth vs. Product-Market Fit.** Do your patent claims cover the competitive advantage customers actually pay for? The test maps each granted claim to a specific revenue-generating feature or capability. Claims that protect technical details irrelevant to your commercial differentiation are dead weight. The strongest portfolios have at least 60% of claims directly aligned with paying-customer value drivers.

**Point 2 — Design-Around Resistance.** How many engineering months would it take a well-funded competitor to achieve the same functional outcome without infringing your claims? If the answer is six weeks, your IP creates no meaningful barrier. If the answer is eighteen months and a fundamentally different technical architecture, you have a moat. Beyond Elevation scores this by mapping the number of independent technical paths to the same commercial result — fewer viable paths means stronger defensibility.

**Point 3 — Geographic Coverage Gaps.** Patents protect you only in jurisdictions where they are granted. A US-only portfolio leaves your European, Asian, and Middle Eastern markets completely exposed. The IP defensibility assessment evaluates whether your filing jurisdictions match your current revenue geography, your three-year expansion plan, and your competitors' manufacturing and distribution footprint.

**Point 4 — Prosecution History Strength.** Every patent has a prosecution file — the documented negotiation between your attorney and the patent examiner. Competitors read this file. If claims were significantly narrowed during prosecution, competitors use prosecution history estoppel to argue that surrendered scope is free to exploit. A strong prosecution history shows claims that held through examination without major concessions.

**Point 5 — Remaining Patent Life.** A patent with three years of remaining life creates less competitive advantage than one with fifteen. The test calculates weighted average remaining life across the portfolio and compares it to the company's growth timeline and projected exit window. If core patents expire before your target exit date, they contribute nothing to acquisition value. The minimum viable threshold is 10 years of remaining life on patents covering primary revenue drivers.

**Point 6 — Licensing and Revenue Optionality.** Can your patents generate income beyond your own products? The test evaluates whether your claims cover technology used by companies outside your direct competitive market. A patent on a novel data preprocessing method built for healthcare might be licensable to financial services, automotive, and manufacturing companies running similar pipelines. This cross-industry optionality transforms a [defensive portfolio into a revenue-generating asset](/blog/posts/patent-licensing-revenue-model/) with licensing income that scales independently of your core business.

**Point 7 — Competitive Landscape Position.** Where does your portfolio sit relative to competitors filing in the same space? The test maps your claims against competitor patent filings, identifies overlap zones and white space, and assesses whether your portfolio creates offensive leverage — the ability to assert against competitors entering your market — or merely defensive cover. The strongest portfolios create both, and the gap between offensive and defensive positioning is where [patent moat strategy](/blog/posts/what-is-a-patent-moat/) separates category leaders from followers.

## What Does an IP Defensibility Assessment Reveal About Valuation?

An IP defensibility assessment directly predicts the valuation premium your IP assets will command in fundraising and exit scenarios. Founders whose portfolios score 5 out of 7 or higher consistently achieve exit multiples 2–4x above comparable companies with weaker IP positions. The mechanism is straightforward: acquirers discount IP they cannot verify as defensible, and investors price unquantified risk as maximum risk.

Hayat Amin showed this in a portfolio restructuring where the founders valued their 11-patent portfolio at $2M. The defensibility assessment revealed that 4 patents had claims too narrow to create competitive barriers, 2 had prosecution histories full of concessions, and 1 covered technology the company had already pivoted away from. The 4 remaining patents — repositioned with broader claims — were valued at $8M by the acquiring company. The assessment told the founders which patents to double down on, which to abandon, and which to refile before the exit conversation.

## How Should Founders Prepare for an IP Defensibility Assessment?

Every founder should run an IP defensibility assessment at three critical moments: before a fundraising round, before any acquisition conversation, and annually as a portfolio management discipline. The assessment is not a one-time event — it is a recurring diagnostic that keeps your IP strategy aligned with your business as markets, competitors, and your own product roadmap evolve.

Hayat Amin reminds founders that the 10.2x funding advantage from holding patents only materialises when the portfolio survives scrutiny. A VC's technical due diligence team will run their own version of this test whether you prepare for it or not. The founders who win better terms are the ones who ran the test first — identified the gaps, fixed the structural weaknesses, and walked into the room with a scored, documented IP position.

Start with the basics. Gather every granted patent, pending application, and provisional filing. Map each set of claims to specific product features and revenue streams. Identify where competitors are filing and where they are not. Then run the seven-point diagnostic. If you score below 5, the remediation plan should start before your next investor meeting.

## FAQ

### What is an IP defensibility assessment?

An IP defensibility assessment is a structured evaluation of your patent portfolio and trade secrets to determine whether they create genuine competitive barriers. It tests claim breadth, design-around resistance, geographic coverage, prosecution history, remaining useful life, licensing optionality, and competitive positioning — the seven dimensions that predict whether your IP holds up under investor scrutiny or [acquisition due diligence](/blog/posts/ip-strategy-startups-guide/).

### How much does an IP defensibility assessment cost?

A comprehensive IP defensibility assessment typically ranges from £5,000 to £25,000 depending on portfolio size, jurisdiction count, and competitive landscape complexity. The return is measurable — founders who remediate gaps identified in the assessment consistently achieve higher valuations in their next funding round or exit negotiation.

### How often should startups run an IP defensibility assessment?

Run a full assessment before every major fundraising round and any acquisition conversation. Run an abbreviated annual review to verify portfolio alignment with your current product roadmap and competitive environment. Markets shift, competitors file new patents, and your technology evolves — an assessment that scored well 18 months ago may have critical gaps today.

### What is the difference between an IP audit and an IP defensibility assessment?

An IP audit is an inventory — it catalogues what you own. An IP defensibility assessment evaluates whether what you own actually creates competitive protection. The audit tells you that you hold 12 patents. The assessment tells you that 4 create meaningful barriers and 8 are consuming maintenance budget without strategic value. Both are necessary, but the defensibility assessment drives the decisions that affect your valuation and exit multiple.

### Can a pre-revenue startup benefit from an IP defensibility assessment?

Yes. Pre-revenue startups with filed patents can be assessed on claim breadth, design-around resistance, prosecution trajectory, and competitive positioning. The assessment identifies which innovations to prioritise for full utility filing and which to protect as trade secrets — saving founders from spending limited IP budget on patents that create no competitive barrier.
