# How Do You Calculate the Value of a Patent? The 3-Number Formula Nobody Teaches

93% of patent owners cannot answer one question: what is your patent worth? Not "a lot." Not "it depends." A number.

Most founders treat patent valuation like a guessing game. They ask their patent attorney — who has no idea, because valuation is not what patent attorneys do. They ask their accountant — who defaults to filing costs, which tells you nothing about commercial value. Or they skip the question entirely and walk into fundraising rounds, licensing negotiations, and M&A conversations without the single number that determines their leverage.

Hayat Amin argues this is the most expensive blind spot in founder-led companies. "Every patent has a calculable value," Hayat Amin says. "The formula is three numbers. Most founders have never seen it because the people advising them — lawyers and accountants — are not trained to run it." Companies with patents are 10.2x more likely to secure early-stage funding, but only if they can defend the number they put on those patents in front of investors.

So how do you calculate the value of a patent? Here is the formula, the math behind each variable, and the multipliers that separate a $50K patent from a $5M one.

## How Do You Calculate the Value of a Patent?

The most reliable way to calculate the value of a patent is the income approach: Patent Value = Addressable Market × Royalty Rate × Remaining Patent Life, discounted to present value. This is the formula institutional investors, acquirers, and licensing firms use — and it is the foundation of every serious patent valuation that [Beyond Elevation](https://beyondelevation.com) delivers.

The income approach works because it ties patent value directly to the revenue the patent can generate. Unlike the cost approach (what you spent to develop and file), which tells you nothing about commercial potential, or the market approach (what comparable patents sold for), which requires transaction data that rarely exists for early-stage patents, the income approach gives you a forward-looking number grounded in economics. For a deeper look at all three methods, see our guide on [IP valuation methods explained](/blog/posts/ip-valuation-methods-explained/).

Here is how each of the three variables works — and where founders consistently get them wrong.

## What Is the Addressable Market for Your Patent Claims?

The addressable market for patent valuation is not your company's TAM — it is the total revenue generated by every product and service that practises your patent claims, across every company that could owe you a licence. This number is almost always larger than founders expect, and it is the single biggest driver of patent value calculation accuracy.

Start by mapping your patent claims to commercial products. If your patent covers a data compression method used in video streaming, your addressable market includes every streaming platform, CDN provider, and video conferencing tool that uses a substantially similar method. Patent claims define the boundary — not your product category, not your vertical, not your current customer list.

Hayat Amin's Patent Mining Method starts here. "Most founders look at their own product and stop. The real value is in the 10 other companies using the same technique in adjacent markets. That is where the licensing revenue sits." One recent engagement uncovered $340M in addressable market across three industries the founder had never considered — all from a single patent family originally filed to protect a $2M product line.

For a software patent, the addressable market typically ranges from $50M to $5B in total revenue covered by the claims. The more broadly your claims read, the larger the market — and the higher the patent value calculation result.

## How Do You Determine the Right Royalty Rate for a Patent Valuation?

The royalty rate is the percentage of addressable market revenue that a licensee would pay to use your patented technology. Industry benchmarks for 2026: software patents command 8–12% of net sales, SaaS and pharma patents sit at 15% or higher, and automotive and hardware patents anchor at 2–4%.

These rates come from actual licence agreements compiled by sources like the Stanzione 2026 data and RoyaltyStat databases. But benchmarks are starting points, not answers. The actual rate depends on the strength of your claims, the competitive alternatives available to the licensee, and how essential your patented technology is to the licensee's product. For a detailed look at the most common royalty heuristic investors use, see our breakdown of [the 25% rule in IP valuation](/blog/posts/25-percent-rule-ip-valuation/).

Hayat Amin's Royalty Stack Framework prices licences against the licensee's gross margin, not just industry averages. "If a licensee makes 70% gross margins on a product that cannot function without your patent, a 4% royalty rate is leaving money on the table," Hayat Amin argues. "You price against the margin the patent enables, not the industry the licensee sits in." This approach consistently produces royalty rates 2–3x higher than the standard benchmarks for patents with strong claim coverage.

The patent licensing market is growing at 7.77% CAGR, reaching a projected $5.48B by 2035. Getting the royalty rate right in your patent value calculation is the difference between underpricing your asset by millions and capturing its true worth.

## Why Does Remaining Patent Life Change the Entire Calculation?

A patent with 18 years of remaining life is worth dramatically more than the same patent with 4 years left — but not in a linear way. Patent value follows a curve that peaks in years 5–12, when the technology is commercially mature and competitors face maximum switching costs, then declines as expiration approaches and licensees wait it out rather than pay.

The discount rate you apply — typically 15–30% for patent assets, depending on enforcement risk and market volatility — compresses future royalty income into present value. A $10M addressable market at a 10% royalty rate over 15 years is not $15M in patent value. Discounted at 20%, it is closer to $4.7M. The discount rate answers one question: how certain is the money?

Patents that have survived IPR challenges, have clean prosecution histories, and cover technology already embedded in commercial products earn lower discount rates and higher present values. Patents that are untested, narrowly claimed, or covering pre-commercial technology get discounted heavily. ## What Makes a Patent Worth More Than the Formula Suggests?

The 3-number formula gives you a base value. Five multipliers can push the real patent value calculation significantly higher: patent family size, litigation track record, standards-essential status, competitive distance, and active licensing revenue. Founders who ignore these multipliers consistently undervalue their portfolios.

A patent family — a cluster of related patents covering the same core invention across multiple jurisdictions and claim variations — is worth more than the sum of its parts. A single patent can be designed around. A family of seven covering the same technology from different angles creates a wall that competitors cannot climb over, under, or around. For a deeper look at how families compound value, see our guide on [patent clustering strategy](/blog/posts/patent-clustering-strategy-moat/).

Litigation track record matters. A patent that has been asserted and survived invalidity challenges in court is worth 3–5x more than an untested patent with identical claims. Every survived challenge is a proof point that the claims hold.

Standards-essential status — where your patent covers technology adopted into an industry standard like Wi-Fi, 5G, or Bluetooth — creates a mandatory licensing market. Every manufacturer implementing the standard needs your licence. And active licensing revenue is the ultimate multiplier. A patent already generating royalties is no longer theoretical — acquirers and investors value it like recurring revenue, often at 8–12x annual royalty income.

Hayat Amin tells the story of a founder who ran the formula on a single patent and estimated its value at $800K. After mapping the full patent family, identifying three adjacent markets practising the claims, and structuring a [licensing programme](/blog/posts/patent-licensing-revenue-model/), the portfolio closed deals totalling $6.2M over 24 months. The formula was right — the founder just used the wrong inputs.

## FAQ

### How much does a patent valuation cost?

A professional patent valuation typically costs $15,000–$50,000 for a focused portfolio, depending on the complexity of the claims and the number of patents being valued. DIY estimates using the 3-number formula provide a rough baseline, but professional valuations add rigorous market data and claim analysis that typically shift the number by 3–5x.

### Can I calculate patent value without a professional?

Yes. The income approach formula — Addressable Market × Royalty Rate × Remaining Life, discounted to present value — gives you a directional estimate. However, the variables require market research and claim mapping that most founders do not have the tools to do rigorously. Use the formula for internal benchmarking, but get a professional valuation before any external negotiation.

### What is the average value of a patent?

There is no meaningful average. Patent values range from effectively zero (for narrowly claimed, commercially irrelevant patents) to hundreds of millions of dollars (for broadly claimed patents in large addressable markets with active licensing programmes). The value depends entirely on the three variables: addressable market, royalty rate, and remaining life.

### How do investors value patents during due diligence?

Investors use a combination of the income approach, comparable transaction analysis, and qualitative assessment of claim strength, prosecution history, and competitive positioning. Beyond Elevation prepares patent valuation reports specifically structured for investor due diligence — presenting the math in the language VCs and acquirers actually use.

### Does a patent increase my company's valuation?

Companies with patents are 10.2x more likely to secure early-stage funding and typically achieve 20–60% higher valuation multiples than comparable companies without IP protection. But the premium depends on the quality, breadth, and commercial relevance of the patents — not just their existence.
