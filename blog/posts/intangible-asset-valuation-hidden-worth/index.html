Ninety percent of the S&P 500's market value is intangible assets. Not buildings. Not equipment. Not inventory. Patents, proprietary data, algorithms, customer relationships, and trade secrets. That is not a rounding error. That is leaving millions on a table you did not even know existed.

What Is Intangible Asset Valuation and Why Should Founders Care?

Intangible asset valuation is the process of assigning a defensible dollar figure to non-physical assets — patents, proprietary datasets, trade secrets, software, brand equity, and documented know-how. Beyond Elevation works with tech and AI founders to surface this hidden value because it directly determines what investors will pay for a stake in your company and what acquirers will pay to own it outright.

Here is the number that should bother you: Ocean Tomo's 2024 study found intangible assets represent 90% of total S&P 500 enterprise value — up from 17% in 1975. The market figured this out decades ago. Most founders still have not.

If you cannot put a number on your intangibles, you are negotiating blind. And negotiating blind against a PE firm or strategic acquirer with a full-time valuation team is not a fair fight.

Why Does the Gap Between Book Value and Market Value Destroy Founder Outcomes?

Your balance sheet shows tangible assets and maybe some capitalised R&D. It does not show the proprietary inference pipeline your team spent 18 months building. It does not show the 4.2 million labelled data points you curated. It does not show the 11 patents covering your core architecture.

When you sit across from a buyer or investor without a formal intangible asset valuation, three things happen:

1. They set the price. If you have not valued your IP, they will — and their number will always be lower than yours. That is not malice. It is leverage. A buyer with a DCF model and a seller with a gut feeling is a negotiation that ends one way every time.

2. You cannot defend your ask. Saying "our technology is worth $20 million" without a third-party valuation is an opinion. Presenting a formal intangible asset valuation backed by income approach, relief-from-royalty, and comparable transaction data is evidence. Investors fund evidence. They discount opinions.

3. You miss licensing revenue entirely. You cannot license what you have not valued. Position Imaging came to Beyond Elevation with 66 patents and zero licensing revenue. After restructuring and formal valuation, those patents became a monetisable portfolio. You do not know what your IP is worth until you measure it — and you cannot sell access to something you cannot price.

What Intangible Assets Actually Drive Valuation in Tech and AI Companies?

Not all intangibles are created equal. Here is where the real value concentrates in AI and tech companies, ranked by typical valuation impact:

Patent portfolios. Companies with patents are 10.2x more likely to secure early-stage funding, according to a study by the European Patent Office. That is not a soft correlation. That is a binary filter. Investors use patent filings as a proxy for genuine innovation versus "me too" implementation. A structured patent portfolio — not random filings, but a deliberate strategy covering core architecture, methods, and applications — creates a defensible moat that directly multiplies enterprise value.

Proprietary data assets. In AI, the model is the commodity. The data is the moat. A curated, labelled, domain-specific dataset that took three years to build cannot be replicated by a competitor in three months no matter how much they spend. DGS came to Beyond Elevation looking to monetise their data assets. Formal valuation turned what they considered an operational byproduct into a revenue-generating strategic asset.

Trade secrets and documented know-how. The training recipes, hyperparameter configurations, deployment playbooks, and evaluation frameworks inside your engineering team's heads are worth real money — but only if they are documented, protected, and valued. Undocumented know-how is not an asset. It is a liability with legs that can walk out the door on a Friday and start at a competitor on Monday.

Software and algorithms. Custom-built tools, proprietary algorithms, and internal platforms that give your team a speed or accuracy advantage over competitors have quantifiable value. The relief-from-royalty method asks: what would a licensee pay to use this? That number is often 5 to 15 percent of the revenue the software enables — and it adds directly to enterprise value.

How Do You Actually Value Intangible Assets?

Three methods. Each serves a different purpose. The best valuations use all three and triangulate.

Income approach. Project the future cash flows attributable to the intangible asset. Discount them back to present value. This works best for patents and data assets with clear revenue linkage. If your patent portfolio protects $8 million in annual revenue and a reasonable royalty rate is 6%, that portfolio is generating $480,000 per year in attributable value — and its present value over the remaining patent life is substantial.

Market approach. Find comparable transactions — what have similar patents, datasets, or trade secret portfolios sold or licensed for? Ktmine, ROL Solutions, and public SEC filings provide benchmarks. This method is the most persuasive to investors because it uses real market evidence, not projections.

Cost approach. Calculate what it would cost to recreate the asset from scratch. This sets the floor value. If your dataset took 14 engineers 2.5 years to build and curate, the replacement cost is north of $5 million before you factor in opportunity cost and the learning curve a competitor would face.

When Should You Get an Intangible Asset Valuation?

Yesterday. But specifically:

Before any fundraise. Walking into a Series A or B with a formal IP and intangible asset valuation shifts the power dynamic. You are no longer asking for a number. You are presenting one — with evidence. Companies that do this consistently close rounds at 15 to 30 percent higher valuations than those that don't.

Before any exit conversation. Acquirers run their own intangible asset valuations during due diligence. If your number is higher than theirs — and it is backed by rigorous methodology — the negotiation starts from your position, not theirs.

Before licensing discussions. You cannot credibly quote a royalty rate without knowing what the underlying asset is worth. Valuation converts "we think this is valuable" into "here is what the market data says this is worth." One of those statements closes deals. The other gets ignored.

After any major R&D milestone. Filed a new patent? Completed a proprietary dataset? Built a novel architecture? The value of your intangible assets just changed. Update the valuation. Founders who track intangible asset value the way they track ARR make better decisions about where to invest, what to protect, and when to monetise.

What is the difference between IP valuation and intangible asset valuation?

IP valuation focuses specifically on intellectual property — patents, trademarks, copyrights, and trade secrets. Intangible asset valuation is broader, covering IP plus other non-physical assets like customer relationships, brand equity, assembled workforce value, and proprietary processes.
